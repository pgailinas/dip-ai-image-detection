# 7. Results & Insights

## Overview

This section presents the experimental results and analytical insights obtained from the Digital Image Processing (DIP)–based AI image detection pipeline. The objective is not only to report classification performance, but also to examine how different feature types, model choices, and dataset combinations influence overall detection behavior and generalization capability.

The experiments evaluate the effectiveness of a handcrafted 26-feature DIP representation derived from gradient-domain, spatial-domain, and frequency-domain image characteristics. Multiple classifiers, feature subsets, and dataset pairings were analyzed to better understand which components contribute most strongly to robust AI-image detection.

The results are organized into the following major analysis categories:

- **Classifier Comparison** — Evaluation of multiple baseline classifiers using stratified cross-validation

- **Final Model Selection and Performance Summary** — Comparison and evaluation of the top-performing tuned classifiers on the independent test set

- **Feature-Level Results and Analysis** — Evaluation of the discriminative strength and standalone predictive capability of individual DIP features

- **Group-Level Results and Analysis** — Comparison of gradient-domain, spatial-domain, and frequency-domain feature groups and their contribution to classification performance

- **Source-Pair Analysis and Generalization Insights** — Evaluation of model robustness across multiple real-versus-AI source pairs, including interpretation of cross-source behavior and generator-independent detection performance

- **Experimental Observations** — Additional findings and practical insights that emerged outside the primary objectives of the major evaluation notebooks

- **Key Takeaways** — Summary of the primary findings, experimental insights, and overall conclusions of the project

Together, these results demonstrate how explicit DIP feature engineering can provide strong classification performance while also offering interpretability and deeper insight into the statistical differences between real and AI-generated imagery.

---

## Classifier Comparison

This experiment evaluated multiple baseline classifiers using the normalized 26-feature DIP representation. All models were assessed using stratified 5-fold cross-validation, with mean ROC-AUC used as the primary ranking metric.

The goal of this stage was to identify which classifier architectures were most effective at separating real and AI-generated images before performing final model tuning and independent test evaluation.

<p align="center">
  <img src="images/classifier-comparison.png" width="700">
</p>
<p align="center"><em>Figure 1: Baseline classifier comparison ranked by mean ROC-AUC</em></p>

The results show that nonlinear classifiers consistently outperformed simpler linear and probabilistic approaches. In particular, the RBF SVM and MLP achieved the strongest overall performance across multiple evaluation metrics, while tree-based ensemble methods also demonstrated strong classification capability.

<p align="center"><em>Table 1: Cross-validation performance summary across all evaluated baseline classifiers</em></p>

| Classifier | Accuracy | Precision | Recall | F1 Score | ROC-AUC |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| RBF SVM | 0.7779 | **0.7723** | 0.7882 | 0.7802 | **0.8608** |
| MLP | **0.7790** | 0.7717 | **0.7925** | **0.7819** | 0.8594 |
| Random Forest | 0.7646 | 0.7690 | 0.7565 | 0.7627 | 0.8465 |
| Extra Trees | 0.7594 | 0.7590 | 0.7606 | 0.7597 | 0.8440 |
| Gradient Boosting | 0.7494 | 0.7462 | 0.7560 | 0.7510 | 0.8312 |
| Linear SVM | 0.7308 | 0.7248 | 0.7444 | 0.7344 | 0.7996 |
| Logistic Regression | 0.7304 | 0.7237 | 0.7454 | 0.7343 | 0.7992 |
| KNN | 0.7240 | 0.7264 | 0.7187 | 0.7224 | 0.7932 |
| Gaussian NB | 0.6508 | 0.6462 | 0.6660 | 0.6558 | 0.7108 |

Several important observations can be made from these results:

* **RBF SVM** achieved the highest overall ROC-AUC, suggesting that nonlinear decision boundaries are highly effective for modeling the statistical separation between real and AI-generated imagery within the DIP feature space.
* **MLP** produced nearly identical performance to the RBF SVM, indicating that the DIP feature representation is also highly compatible with neural-network-based classification. Although the RBF SVM achieved the highest ROC-AUC, the MLP demonstrated comparable overall performance while also producing the highest F1 score and recall among all evaluated classifiers.
* **Tree ensemble methods** (Random Forest, Extra Trees, and Gradient Boosting) also demonstrated strong performance, indicating that many DIP features contain informative nonlinear interactions that can be effectively exploited through recursive feature partitioning.
* **Linear models** such as Logistic Regression and Linear SVM performed noticeably worse, suggesting that the separation between real and synthetic imagery is not purely linearly separable.
* **Gaussian Naive Bayes** produced the weakest performance, likely because the DIP features violate the model’s independence assumptions.

Although the RBF SVM achieved the highest untuned ROC-AUC, both the RBF SVM and MLP demonstrated strong and stable performance and were therefore selected for subsequent tuning and final evaluation experiments.

Overall, the classifier comparison results demonstrate that the handcrafted DIP feature representation contains strong discriminative information capable of supporting robust classification across multiple nonlinear learning approaches.

## Final Model Selection and Performance Summary

Following baseline classifier comparison and hyperparameter tuning, the final evaluation stage focused on the two strongest candidate classifiers:

* **RBF SVM**
* **MLP (Multi-Layer Perceptron)**

Both models were trained using the normalized 26-feature DIP representation and evaluated using an independent test dataset that was completely isolated from training and cross-validation procedures. This separation ensured that the final evaluation reflected true generalization performance without optimization bias or data leakage.

The final evaluation metrics included:

* **Accuracy** — overall classification correctness
* **Precision** — reliability of AI-generated image predictions
* **Recall** — ability to correctly detect AI-generated images
* **F1-score** — balanced measure of precision and recall
* **ROC-AUC** — threshold-independent measure of class separability

Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) analysis was used as the primary evaluation method because it measures classifier behavior across all decision thresholds rather than at a single operating point.

<p align="center">
  <img src="images/roc-curve-comparison.png" width="700">
</p>
<p align="center"><em>Figure: ROC curve comparison on the independent test set</em></p>

The final test-set ROC-AUC values were:

| Model | ROC-AUC |
|---|---:|
| RBF SVM | 0.8693 |
| MLP | 0.8374 |

The ROC curves demonstrate that both classifiers substantially outperform random classification behavior, confirming that the handcrafted DIP feature representation captures meaningful statistical differences between real and AI-generated imagery.

Several important observations emerge from the final evaluation:

* The **RBF SVM** achieved the highest overall ROC-AUC, indicating stronger threshold-independent separability on the independent test dataset.
* The **MLP** also demonstrated strong and stable classification performance across the full false-positive-rate range.
* Both models generalized effectively on unseen data, suggesting that the DIP feature representation captures robust statistical characteristics rather than dataset-specific artifacts.
* The smooth ROC trajectories indicate consistent classifier behavior rather than isolated decision-region performance.

Although the RBF SVM produced the strongest ROC-AUC, the tuned MLP remained highly competitive and was selected as the primary model for subsequent feature-analysis and source-pair experiments because of its strong overall metric balance and its ability to model complex nonlinear relationships among gradient, spatial, and frequency-domain features.

Overall, the final evaluation results demonstrate that handcrafted DIP features can support robust AI-image detection performance even without deep end-to-end image learning architectures.

## Feature-Level Results and Analysis

Feature-level analysis was performed to evaluate the standalone discriminative capability of each individual DIP feature within the 26-feature representation. In this experiment, classifiers were trained using one feature at a time, and the resulting ROC-AUC values were compared against the full multi-feature baseline model.

The objective of this analysis was to better understand:

* Which features provide the strongest independent predictive power
* Which features contribute primarily through interaction with other features
* How different statistical image characteristics influence AI-image detection performance

<p align="center">
  <img src="images/single-feature-barchart.png" width="800">
</p>
<p align="center"><em>Figure: Single-feature ROC-AUC performance compared to the full-feature baseline model</em></p>

The results show that several individual DIP features exhibit strong standalone classification capability, although none individually matched the performance of the complete combined feature representation.

Among the strongest standalone features were:

| Feature | Domain |
|---|---|
| Laplacian Variance | Spatial / Texture |
| High Frequency Energy Ratio | Frequency |
| Log Spectrum Std | Frequency |
| Noise Residual Energy | Spatial |
| Max Gradient | Gradient |

Several important observations emerge from this analysis:

* **Frequency-domain features** consistently ranked among the strongest individual predictors, suggesting that AI-generated imagery contains detectable spectral irregularities and abnormal frequency distributions.
* **Gradient-based features** also demonstrated strong performance, indicating that edge structure and local directional statistics differ measurably between real and synthetic images.
* **Spatial texture measures** such as Laplacian variance and noise residual energy contributed substantial discriminative information, likely capturing differences in fine-scale texture consistency and image smoothness.
* No single feature approached the performance of the full combined model, reinforcing the importance of multi-feature integration across multiple image-analysis domains.
* Some lower-ranked features may still contribute indirectly through complementary interactions with other features inside the final classifier.

The dashed horizontal line in the figure represents the full-feature baseline ROC-AUC, illustrating the performance gain achieved when all DIP features are combined into a unified representation.

Overall, the feature-level analysis demonstrates that AI-generated image artifacts are distributed across multiple statistical image domains rather than isolated to a single measurable characteristic. This supports the use of a diverse handcrafted DIP feature set for robust generator-independent detection.

## Group-Level Results and Analysis

Group-level analysis was performed to evaluate the relative contribution of the three major DIP feature domains:

* **Gradient-Based Features**
* **Spatial-Domain Features**
* **Frequency-Domain Features**

Each feature group was evaluated independently using the same classification pipeline and compared against the complete 26-feature model. This experiment was designed to determine how much discriminative information is contained within each image-analysis domain and how strongly the groups complement one another when combined.

<p align="center">
  <img src="images/feature-group-vs-full-model.png" width="850">
</p>
<p align="center"><em>Figure: Performance comparison between individual feature groups and the full combined model</em></p>

The results show that all three feature domains contribute meaningful classification information, although their standalone performance varies considerably.

Several important observations emerge from the comparison:

* The **spatial-domain feature group** achieved the strongest standalone performance across most evaluation metrics, suggesting that texture consistency, entropy behavior, and local image structure provide highly informative cues for distinguishing real and synthetic imagery.
* The **frequency-domain feature group** also demonstrated strong performance, reinforcing the observation that AI-generated images often exhibit detectable spectral artifacts and abnormal frequency distributions.
* The **gradient-based feature group** produced the weakest standalone performance, although it still provided meaningful predictive capability and contributed additional complementary information to the full model.
* The **full combined model** substantially outperformed all isolated feature groups across every evaluation metric, demonstrating the value of integrating multiple image-analysis domains into a unified representation.

The comparison also highlights the complementary nature of the three feature categories:

| Feature Group | Primary Information Captured |
|---|---|
| Gradient-Based | Edge structure, directional statistics, local transitions |
| Spatial-Domain | Texture consistency, entropy, intensity variation |
| Frequency-Domain | Spectral energy distribution and frequency irregularities |

The ROC-AUC results further emphasize the importance of feature integration. While the strongest isolated group achieved meaningful separability, the full model achieved the highest overall ROC-AUC by leveraging information distributed across all three domains simultaneously.

Overall, the group-level analysis demonstrates that AI-generated image artifacts are multidimensional in nature. No single feature domain fully captures all synthetic-image characteristics, but the combination of gradient, spatial, and frequency-domain statistics produces a substantially more robust and generalized detection representation.

## Source-Pair Analysis and Generalization Insights

To evaluate model robustness and cross-source generalization behavior, a total of **9 source-pair experiments** were conducted using all combinations of:

* **3 real-image datasets**
  * ImageNet
  * MS COCO
  * OpenImages

* **3 AI-generated datasets**
  * DiffusionDB
  * SDXL
  * Midjourney

Each experiment trained and evaluated a classifier using a specific real-versus-AI dataset pairing. This analysis was designed to determine how strongly detection performance depends on dataset composition and whether the handcrafted DIP feature representation generalizes consistently across different image sources and generation methods.

The table below summarizes the final evaluation metrics for all source-pair experiments.

| Source Pair | Accuracy | Precision | Recall | F1 Score | ROC-AUC |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| coco_sdxl | 0.9492 | 0.9455 | 0.9533 | 0.9494 | 0.9881 |
| imgn_sdxl | 0.9500 | 0.9485 | 0.9517 | 0.9501 | 0.9866 |
| open_sdxl | 0.9275 | 0.9226 | 0.9333 | 0.9279 | 0.9761 |
| imgn_diff | 0.8650 | 0.8724 | 0.8550 | 0.8636 | 0.9388 |
| imgn_midj | 0.8517 | 0.8448 | 0.8617 | 0.8531 | 0.9323 |
| coco_diff | 0.8400 | 0.8411 | 0.8383 | 0.8397 | 0.9236 |
| coco_midj | 0.8317 | 0.8295 | 0.8350 | 0.8322 | 0.9204 |
| open_diff | 0.8125 | 0.8092 | 0.8175 | 0.8133 | 0.9031 |
| open_midj | 0.7983 | 0.7940 | 0.8033 | 0.7986 | 0.8917 |

Several important trends emerge from the source-pair experiments:

* **SDXL-based source pairs** consistently achieved the strongest performance, with ROC-AUC values approaching 0.99 in some cases.
* **DiffusionDB** and **Midjourney** source pairs were generally more difficult to classify, suggesting that their generated images contain fewer easily detectable statistical artifacts.
* Source pairs involving **OpenImages** typically produced lower performance than ImageNet or COCO pairings, likely due to increased variability and diversity within the OpenImages dataset.
* Despite performance variation across source combinations, all source-pair experiments substantially outperformed random classification behavior, demonstrating meaningful cross-source generalization capability.

ROC curve analysis provides a more detailed view of classifier behavior across decision thresholds.

The ROC curves reveal several additional insights:

* The strongest-performing source pairs rise rapidly toward the upper-left corner of the plot, indicating excellent separability between real and AI-generated imagery.
* Lower-performing source pairs exhibit smoother and more gradual ROC trajectories, reflecting increased overlap between real and synthetic feature distributions.
* The relatively consistent separation across all curves suggests that the DIP feature representation captures generalized image statistics rather than overfitting to a single generator or dataset source.

The aggregated model trained on **all real versus all AI datasets** achieved a ROC-AUC of approximately 0.86, indicating that generator-independent detection remains feasible even when combining highly diverse data sources.

Overall, the source-pair analysis demonstrates that dataset composition significantly influences classification difficulty, but also confirms that the handcrafted DIP feature representation maintains strong robustness across multiple real-image sources and AI-generation methods.

The source-pair experiments reveal one of the most important findings of this project:

> **Detection performance varies significantly across dataset and generator combinations.**

Although the overall DIP-based detection pipeline achieved strong aggregate performance, the source-pair analysis demonstrated that some real-versus-AI combinations are substantially easier to separate than others. This variation provides important insight into the statistical diversity of both real-image datasets and modern AI image generators.

Several key observations emerge from the results:

* Some source pairs exhibited extremely strong separability, achieving ROC-AUC values approaching 0.99.
* Other dataset combinations produced noticeably lower performance, indicating greater overlap between real and synthetic feature distributions.
* AI generators do not produce identical statistical artifacts; different generation methods leave different detectable signatures within gradient, spatial, and frequency-domain representations.
* Real-image datasets also vary considerably in texture diversity, image composition, noise characteristics, and compression behavior, all of which influence classification difficulty.

The experiments further suggest that frequency-domain and texture-related statistics are particularly sensitive to generator-specific behavior. In some cases, AI-generated images contained strong spectral irregularities or texture inconsistencies that were highly separable from real imagery. In other cases, the synthetic images more closely matched the statistical properties of the corresponding real-image dataset, reducing classification margin.

These findings reinforce several important conclusions regarding AI-image detection:

* High overall accuracy alone does not guarantee reliable performance across all datasets or generators.
* Cross-source evaluation is essential for understanding true model robustness.
* Detection systems trained on limited datasets may unintentionally overfit to generator-specific artifacts.
* Strong generalization requires both diverse training data and feature representations capable of capturing multiple forms of statistical evidence.

The results also support one of the central motivations behind the project: using a handcrafted multi-domain DIP feature representation rather than relying exclusively on narrow source-specific patterns. Because the proposed feature vector integrates gradient, spatial, and frequency-domain information, the model is able to maintain meaningful performance even across challenging cross-source conditions.

Overall, the generalization analysis demonstrates that AI-image detection should be treated as a robustness problem rather than a single-dataset classification problem. Evaluating performance across multiple real-image sources and multiple AI generators is critical for developing detection systems that remain effective as generation methods continue to evolve.

## Key Takeaways

The experimental results demonstrate that handcrafted Digital Image Processing (DIP) features provide a strong and interpretable foundation for AI-generated image detection. By combining gradient-based, spatial-domain, and frequency-domain statistics into a unified feature representation, the proposed pipeline achieved consistently strong classification performance across multiple models, datasets, and evaluation scenarios.

Several major conclusions emerged from the analysis:

* **DIP-based feature engineering is highly effective** for distinguishing real and AI-generated imagery, even without end-to-end deep image feature extraction.
* **Nonlinear classifiers**, particularly RBF SVM and MLP architectures, are well suited for modeling the complex statistical relationships present within the DIP feature space.
* **Multi-domain feature integration improves robustness**, with the combined feature representation consistently outperforming isolated feature groups and individual standalone features.
* **Frequency-domain and texture-related features** provided especially strong discriminative capability, suggesting that synthetic imagery often contains measurable spectral and structural irregularities.
* **Dataset composition strongly influences performance**, indicating that both real-image diversity and AI generator characteristics affect classification difficulty.
* **Source-pair analysis is critical** for evaluating true generalization behavior and avoiding overly optimistic conclusions based on limited datasets.
* **ROC curve analysis provides deeper insight** into classifier behavior than single-value metrics alone by revealing performance across the full decision-threshold range.

Overall, the results support the central hypothesis of this project:

> A carefully designed handcrafted DIP feature representation can provide robust and interpretable AI-image detection performance across multiple datasets and generation methods.

The project further demonstrates that generator-independent detection requires both diverse evaluation methodology and feature representations capable of capturing statistical evidence across multiple image-analysis domains simultaneously.
